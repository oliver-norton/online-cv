QUANTIFYING POLARISATION ON A LARGE SCALE USING MACHINE LEARNING AND NETWORKS ANALYSIS

Political polarisation can harm democracy by causing division, gridlock, and limited voter choices, making it important to measure accurately. My research develops a new method to study polarisation in multiparty systems by analysing Twitter data, focusing on the 2022 French presidential election. The findings show that while overall polarisation is low, far-right supporters express more extreme views, and politically active individuals tend to hold stronger opinions, offering insights into public opinion patterns.

Goals:

In my thesis I sought to:

  • Build a relevant text-based dataset of the 2022 French elections
  • Quantify political polarisation using this dataset

Dataset

A collection of over 5 million Tweets and 16 million retweets that I collected from a commercial API, Radarly. The period of time was a few weeks in April 2022.

Summary/Highlights

Data collection

To collect the data I used a boolean query method based on key words related to the elections. A boolean search might be: “election” AND “france” OR “macron”. To identify relevant keywords, and expand my dataset, I used a method called computer assisted keyword expansion, which uses a lasso regression to predict which keywords were topic relevant based on log-likelihood.

Quantifying polarisation

I quantified polarisation using a type of variance called total variation, which is variance between not just two variables, but multiple. In this case, I was measuring the variance between the 4 main political parties. Two of these are those led by Marine Le Pen and President Emmanuel Macron respectively.
